    I have my prediction algorithm working, using the full text as training data.

    In order to optimize / improve I took a baseline on "how good the prediction is".
    So I took an arbitrary piece of text : http://www.gutenberg.org/cache/epub/47448/pg47448.txt
    and cleaned it, and converted it into a vector of words (removing numbers, punctuation, white-space, keeping ascii only)

    This gave me ~ 62000 words. Then I run my prediction on all of the words and for each word using the word itself and the next 1,2,3,4,5,7,8,9 words as input phrase for prediction.
    For all those 1-10 grams I run then my prediction algorithm.
    It took a while (some hours), but at the end it  made
    62000 * 10  = 620000 predictions.

    Out of that 13 % where predicted correctly.
    Is this an average / a lot / very little ?

    I believe, I have used so far the most simple approach:
    - removing numbers, punctuation, non ascii from corpus
     -Just counting of ngrams (1-5),
     - removing n-grams with frequency of "1" (per file (corpus was split into 100 files))
    - simplest back track if n-gram not found
    - simplest formula to calculate probability (highest "count" in training text with highest "n" wins)

    We should keep in mind, that an algorithm which always predicts the word "the", predicts already correctly in 4% of the cases......

    Source: http://www.wordfrequency.info/free.asp?s=y

    Anybody has its own numbers on this ?

testAccuracy <- function(dts,words){
  word.count=length(words)-11
  correct <- 0
  predictions <- 0
  for (i in 1:word.count) {
    print(paste("word:",i,"/",word.count))
    for (j in 0:10) {
      ngram <- paste(words[i:(i+j)],collapse =" ")
      predicted <- predictNext(dts,ngram)
      predictions <- predictions + 1
      if (predicted == words[i+j+1]) {
        correct <- correct + 1
        print(paste("                   Succes: ",correct / predictions * 100))
      }
    }
  }
  
}

    @Peggy
    Aha I see now... indeed, with a missing unigram you got N(*w*) as zero in denominator.
    I created unigram probability table not from parsing the raw text, but according to the Knesser-Ney from bigram table:
    P(w) = N(*w) / N(**) - you can just pass through bigrams and the probability of unigram w is proportional to the nubmer of words preceeding this unigram in bigrams table.
    This way you extract unigrams from bigrams, because the main point of KN smoothing is taking the preceeding words into the account.

    It might happen that a bigram is missing in the trigram table entry, but in that case I simply skip it. I regard this as a minority problem, because if it was not good to make it into the trigrams, it is not significant in the context of KN (which means that this bigram does not have sufficient number of different preceeding words to have a meaningful probability in comparison with other trigrams)

    I see KN way as a quite good self-cleaning model :)

    Juraj VyskočilSignature Track· 6 days ago 
    The trimming might have done it. Try to check N1 entries before the trimming and we will see.
    Actually, I do not log my probabilities, because in fact you do not have to do online computing of P...
    All I need is to compute all the continuation probabilities for all the N-grams. Then I can just sort them alphabeticaly - all but the last column (with sorting priority from left to right:  1. column is sorted from A to Z, and subsequent columns are sorted in groups by individual words from previous column), and the last column (last words in ngram) I sort by the smoothed continuation probability computed in previous steps.

    This way when I do have w1 and w2 (I use 3-grams), I will predict the next word by quick searching the range of w1 in the first (alphabetically sorted) column, and then within this range I search the range of w2 in the second (sorted as well) column. Now I get range for the third column, from which I choose the most probable words (I use 3-words suggestions). Because the 3rd column is already sorted by the probability, I just take first three words from the 3rd column and that is my prediction of three most probable words. 

    Thus, not only I do not log my probabilities, but on the final step I even do not store them in my model. I compute them with the KN smoothing, then use them to sort the last column, and then get rid of them. I do not compute anything in prediction phase, I just make two lookups in sorted columns, which is pretty quick.

    I tested accuracy of the back-off algorithm as function of the length of the largest n-gram. This was done on the twitter corpus by k-fold cross validation. My results show no gain in accuracy above n=4. For n=4 to 6 the prediction accuracy was 14%. For n=3 the accuracy was 11%. Then it fell off sharply below that.

    Finally, I tested the back-off on a set of n-grams generated from corpus with stop words stripped out. It performed poorly. Accuracy was 7%. Achieving better accuracy than the simple back-off seems to be very challenging. I'm looking into approaches to combine the back-off with some context based prediction.     One of the measures mentioned in the Jurafsky textbook is perplexity, which appears to be the geometric mean of the product of reciprocal probabilities:

    PP(W)=1ΠNk=1P(wk)−−−−−−−−−√N

    where W is a sequence of N words, w1, w2, … wN and P(wk) is the probability of word wk appearing in its position according to some model.

    My initial thoughts are to find some sort of cost function (say perplexity) and then:

    • Set linear coefficients λ1, λ2, …, λm equal (assuming that we wish to incorporate m probability producing models)
    • Take in a short sequence of words from the corpus holdout data.
    • Calculate the cost based on the model that incorporates all the n-gram models (i.e., with the current weights) - this can be seen as the overall model cost.
    • For each of the models we want to incorporate (unigram, bigram, etc.) calculate the cost based on the probabilities the model assigns the words that actually appeared (based on whatever information they need, e.g. the preceding word for the bigram model); the unigram model will basically predict a sequence of the most common token ("the"-"the"-"the"-... N times) and generally assigns very low probabilities to other tokens.
    • Compare the costs of the different models against the overall cost and "reward/penalise" the respective λ's by an amount proportional to any reduction in cost.
    • Renormalise the λ's so that they sum to 1.
    • Feed in the next short sequence and repeat.
    • Hope that there isn't anything fundamentally wrong with this idea and that the λ's will actually converge to a useful set of values... :)
