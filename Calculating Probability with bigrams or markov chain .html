    Nicole GoebelSignature Track· 22 days ago 
    After watching the Stanford NLP videos on ngram probabilities more than once, I am somewhat confused as to how to calculate the probabilities (which I plan to do 'on the fly'). I was hoping someone could  clarify which of the two methods is correct (or best?) - if I have even implemented them correctly?

    Let's use a sentence like "I like to eat at"

    1. Bigram probabilities:  the probability for the sentence above using bigram probabilities is calculated as:

        Pr("I like to eat at") = Pr(like | I) * Pr(to | like) * Pr(eat | to) * Pr(at | eat)

    (and, of course I would use logs of each probability and add them up)

    OR, are we meant to use the Markov Chain Rule, in which case the probabilities would be calculated as:
        
        Pr("I like to eat at") = Pr(I) * Pr(like | I) * Pr(to | I like) * Pr(eat | I like to) * Pr(at | I like to eat)

    Thanks in advance for any insight!

    Maher HarbSignature Track· 22 days ago 

    Short answer, first expression is correct.

    An n-gram language model assumes (as an approximation) that only the n-1 preceding words are important for prediction. Thus, in your bigram example the joint probability would be:

    Pr("I like to eat at") = Pr(I | ^) * Pr(like | I) * Pr(to | like) * Pr(eat | to) * Pr(at | eat)

    (Note, ^ = start of sentence)

    But you don’t really need to calculate such joint probability for the prediction model. You only need to consider the last n-1 words to predict the next word. Calculating the joint probability of the full sentence is useful when evaluating the accuracy of the model on an out-of-sample text.

    Maher HarbSignature Track· 21 days ago 
    @Nicole

    Yes, your description is correct. You don't need the probabilities to implement the back-off algorithm (start with 4-gram then move to 3-gram if the 4-gram was inadequate, and so on...). Frequencies will do the job. 

    Probabilities are important if your algorithm combines results from different length grams. Say you like to assign 70% weight on the 4-gram prediction and 30% weight on the 3-gram. Using frequencies in this case will not work because the shorter length grams will always be more frequent. Thus, you would calculate probabilities before weighting and combining the predictions.
